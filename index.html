TRAVEL IN JIM CROW AMERICA

by Tariq Achor Zyad

DEC. 2nd, 2020

The Great Migration is a history of trailblazing travel, in which social exclusion and marginalization compelled African-Americans to embark upon unfamiliar paths as expatriates and refugees in their own country.

Departing Plight

Often weighed down with pounds of luggage, migrants devoted themselves to reaching the “promised land." High hopes for better work and prosperity spurred on the mass movement of African-Americans from the south to the north and west. Black travelers en masse hit the road in search of better opportunities and the escape of oppression. On one-way tickets and cautious itineraries, black migrants were pioneering paths refused to them by deep-seated racism

The majority of African-Americans living in the south were impoverished under debt bondage. In 1930, only ten percent of southern blacks worked on land that they owned.2 Extreme poverty was rampant in southern black communities.3 One black sharecropper wrote: “The conditions are horrible here with us. they wont give us anyhing to do & say that we wont need anything but something to eat & wont give us anything for what we do & wants us to stay here.”4 Under the sharecropping system African-Americans were living destitute lives of servitude with meagre pay. Child labor was commonplace in black communities. In many cases black families depended on child labor. A tenant farmer from Georgia recalled that the “[only] reason families survived they put their money together … five or six of us children that were working and all of us were working for twenty-five cents a day,”5 Migrants were leaving lives of laborious poverty determined by institutional racial discrimination.

Other African-Americans fled the south from persecution, and racial terror. Pervasive lynchings, murders, and violent threats compelled a number of migrants to travel in search of refuge. Between 1877 and 1950, over 4,000 lynchings of African-Americans occurred in the south.6 Victims would be hanged, burned, and tortured in consequence of minor offenses and dubious rumors.7 When African-Americans spoke out against injustice they risked being murdered. For instance, in 1918, Elton Mitchell, a black farmer from Arkansas who refused to work without pay, was butchered by resentful whites.8 Such killings continued for decades. Racial terror was an inescapable reality for blacks continuing to live in the south. Thus, many African-Americans looked towards the "promised land" and took on travel to find refuge.

Whether employment or refuge were the ambitions of individual migrants, all black migrants ultimately shared a common experience of traveling with the weight of social exclusion. Color was the determining factor in the comfort and convenience of their travels.

The Enduring Paths of Black Travelers

Under the constraints of Jim Crow, black travelers had to navigate their way out of the south with great discretion. De jure and de facto racism placed substantial roadblocks in the paths of blacks fleeing oppression. Limited access to services such as accommodation, transportation, dining and health facilities imposed a great challenge onto African-American travelers. Many migrants were dependent upon word of mouth to find the nearest place to stay.10 Hotels were frequently unavailable to black travelers.11 African-Americans were also subjected to inconvenient transportation services. Segregated transportation often confined black travelers to congested stations and vehicles. The service of black passengers came second to that of their white counterparts. In Benjamin E. May’s recount of his travels, Mays stated that: “Usually the segregated window to serve Negroes was neglected as long as white people were waiting to be served on the other side.”12 As a result, black ticket lines were regularly left under-served.13 Such neglect of black passengers reflected white society’s unabashed disregard for black travelers.

Throughout the Great Migration, migrants faced arduous journeys across the far-flung reaches of the country. Long haul travel was the common mode of transit for blacks fleeing the south. Black train passengers spent hours or even days crammed with the discomfort of Jim Crow cars.14 Passengers in Jim Crow cars were congested into smoke-filled cars with little access to water for extended periods of time.15 For miles black travelers endured the harsh conditions of jam-packed train rides with poor-ventilation and sweltering engine heat.16 Migrants had no choice but to put their faith in the precarious train-cars that were purposefully neglected. The railroads served as key passageways for early migrants travelling to northern and western cities such as Detroit, Los Angeles, New York, and Chicago.17 With great perseverance, black migrants trailblazed their way to freedom, no matter the taxing toll of institutional racism.

The itineraries of black travelers were subject to boundless inconveniences specifically targeting them on the basis of color and race. African-American travelers commonly had to factor in the nuisance of southern law enforcement cracking down on black migration.“To circumvent authorities, African Americans might buy tickets to smaller stations—which were not so heavily guarded, and where they were less likely to be recognized—and there purchase the fare to their final destination.”18 Jim Crow complicated the routes of black travelers with unnecessary layovers, stopovers and train-tickets. All while on a limited budget. “They might also walk many miles to such stations.”19 Black travelers were forced off the beaten path just to guarantee the safety of their travels. Otherwise, they could find themselves with hefty delays, abrupt cancellations, harassment, or even detainment. For instance, one train leaving Brookhaven, Mississippi with 125 black migrants was delayed for three days.20 Other incidents involved black passengers being snatched off of trains; as one black man told Journal of Negro History in 1919, "in spite of all this, they are leaving every day and every night."21 The travel plans of African-Americans in the Great Migration were tenuous to the overbearing grasp of Jim Crow.

Persistence to Enter "the Promised Land"

Given the uncertainty of travel as an African-American, a series of travel guides called The Green Books debuted in 1936 to cater to black travelers and their needs. Their books provided information that could help streamline the itineraries of migrants as they move their way through foreign towns. The three main priorities of The Green Books were to keep black travelers from “running into difficulties, embarrassments and to make his trips more enjoyable.”22 Not only were unexpected mishaps taxing on black travelers but also the humiliation in segregated spaces.The Green Books were a medium in which black journalism helped support black travelers as they face discrimination and humiliation on the road. By advertising specific restaurants and accommodations that accepted black patrons, The Green Books were guiding the African-American migrant community with compassionate precaution. In addition, the guides included tips on preparing travel, such as the page in the 1937 edition advising extra precautions for motorists.23 From an excerpt of the 1949 edition of The Green Book, the company made a statement regarding the nature of their business:

There will be a day sometime in the near future when this guide will not have to be published. That is when we as a race will have equal opportunities and privileges in the United States. It will be a great day for us to suspend this publication for then we can go wherever we please, and without embarrassment. But until that time comes we shall continue to publish this information for your convenience each year.24

Their publications were an essential service for insuring that more African-Americans travelling north would have the know-how required for their harsh journeys.

Despite many hardships, travel was a liberating force for African-Americans. The resilient pursuit of freedom by migrants settled them into the workforce, urban communities and redefined their place in American society. African-American migrants ventured into the unknown in an attempt to find autonomy over their own lives, whether that meant finding safety or financial stability. Though the Jim Crow system was a national issue, the courageous steps taken by migrants out of the south were significant steps toward black liberation.
